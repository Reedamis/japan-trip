Trip 1 — Alsace → Baden-Baden → Salzburg

The thermal corridor. Fairy-tale Alsatian wine villages and kougelhopf bakeries give way to Baden-Baden — one of Europe's great spa cities, built on Roman hot springs — then east into Salzburg, Austria's most beautiful small city, ringed by Alpine hiking trails and a short train ride from the legendary Bad Gastein thermal resort. The whole trip flows east along a natural geographic arc.

PDX to Basel (BSL) or Strasbourg (SXB) via Frankfurt or Zürich. Basel EuroAirport is the easier entry — it's actually on the French side of the border and serves France, Switzerland, and Germany. From the airport, Strasbourg is 1h by train, Colmar is 45 min.

Strasbourg
Friday–Saturday, Days 2–3 🌙🌙

Your base for Alsace. Strasbourg is a canal city of half-timbered houses, a soaring Gothic cathedral, and warm bouchons (traditional Alsatian restaurants) on every corner. The old town — Grande Île — is a UNESCO-listed island surrounded by canals. January is cold and quiet, the Christmas market crowds are gone, and the city feels genuinely local. Excellent bakeries on every block.

✈️ Arriving via Basel airport + train, or direct into SXB

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
The Bakeries — This Is Why You're Here
🥐
Kougelhopf

The Alsatian crown pastry. A ring-shaped brioche studded with raisins and almonds, dusted with powdered sugar. Every boulangerie makes one. Eat it warm, with butter, at the counter.

🥨
Bretzel (Alsatian pretzel)

Alsace is the pretzel's homeland. Soft, chewy, with a proper lye crust and coarse salt. Nothing like what you get at a stadium. Pick one up from any boulangerie for the walk.

🍞
Pain d'épices (spiced bread)

Alsatian spiced gingerbread — denser and more complex than what you might know. Often flavoured with anise, cinnamon, and honey. Found in every bakery and market stall.

Restaurant Food
🥗
Tarte flambée (Flammekueche)

Alsace's answer to pizza — thin crispy dough, crème fraîche, lardons, and onions. Order multiples, they're thin and addictive. The definitive bouchon dish.

🥬
Choucroute garnie

Alsatian sauerkraut piled with pork knuckle, sausages, and potatoes. A proper winter dish. Pairs with a glass of Alsatian Riesling.

🍷
Alsatian wine bars

Riesling, Gewurztraminer, Pinot Gris — Alsace whites are some of France's best. Wine bars in the Grande Île serve them by the glass alongside small plates.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
⛪
Strasbourg Cathedral

One of the tallest medieval buildings in the world. The Gothic facade took 250 years to build and is extraordinary up close. Climb to the platform (332 steps) for views across the Rhine plain into Germany.

🏘️
La Petite France

The most beautiful corner of Strasbourg — half-timbered houses reflected in still canals, lock gates, weir walkways. Empty in January. The kind of place where you stop walking and just stand there.

🖼️
Musée Alsacien

Three connected 17th-century townhouses turned folk museum. Covers Alsatian craft traditions, furniture, costumes, and religious objects. Small, warm, and deeply local — perfect for a cold afternoon.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Les Bains Municipaux

Strasbourg's historic municipal baths — an Art Nouveau building from 1908. Hot pools, steam rooms, locals-only vibe. Very affordable, zero pretension. This is what bathhouse culture actually looks like before it becomes a luxury brand. Tattoo policy: relaxed.

♨️
Niederbronn-les-Bains (40 min north)

A small spa town built around Roman-origin thermal springs. The whole village exists because of the baths. Quiet, genuinely charming, and a great half-day excursion from Strasbourg. The springs have been in use since the 1st century AD.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Vosges Mountains day hikes

The Vosges ridge runs directly west of Strasbourg. Round forested hills with clear trails, viewpoints over the Rhine plain, and old castle ruins at the tops. January hiking is crisp and uncrowded — bring microspikes if there's been snow. The GR5 trail runs the whole length.

🏰
Haut-Kœnigsbourg Castle hike

A medieval castle perched on a Vosges summit at 755m. Combine the castle visit with a short hike through the forest below it. Views extend across the Rhine to the Black Forest on clear days.

Alsace Wine Villages
Sunday, Day 4 🌙 — Day Trip from Strasbourg

The Route des Vins d'Alsace runs south through a string of medieval wine villages so pretty they look fictional. Half-timbered houses, cobbled lanes, geranium-lined in summer and frost-quiet in January. Each village is tiny and takes about an hour to walk properly. Hit two or three in a day.

🚗 Rent a car for the day from Strasbourg — villages are 15–30 min apart along the route.

🗺️ Villages

▼
🏘️
Riquewihr

A walled village almost unchanged since the 16th century. No cars inside the walls. Every building is half-timbered, every street is cobblestone. Wine shops and excellent pâtisseries. In January it is practically yours alone.

🏘️
Eguisheim

Built in concentric rings around a castle courtyard. One of France's officially designated "most beautiful villages." Tiny, circular, and extremely charming. The boulangerie here is exceptional.

🏘️
Kaysersberg

A slightly larger village with a ruined castle on the hill above it, a fortified bridge over the river, and some of the best pâtisseries on the entire wine route. Good lunch stop.

🏙️
Colmar

The main hub of the route — a small city rather than a village, but worth a few hours. "Little Venice" canal district, excellent covered market, and more bakeries per block than seems reasonable.

🥐 Bakeries Along the Route

▼
🍰
Tarte aux quetsches (plum tart)

The Alsatian regional fruit tart — a short pastry shell with local quetsche plums. January versions use preserved fruit. Found in every village pâtisserie along the route.

🍪
Bredele (Alsatian butter cookies)

Small spiced butter cookies in dozens of shapes — still around in January as boulangeries work through their winter stock. Anise, cinnamon, almond. Buy a bag for the drive.

Baden-Baden
Monday–Tuesday, Days 5–6 🌙🌙

Technically Germany, but it fits so naturally into this arc that skipping it would be a mistake. Baden-Baden is one of Europe's great spa cities — built on Roman thermal springs that have been in continuous use since the 1st century. It's small, elegant, set in the Black Forest hills, and the two bath complexes here (Caracalla and Friedrichsbad) are genuinely world-class. Napoleon III came here. Brahms composed here. In January it's quiet, affordable, and exactly what you're looking for.

🚂 Train from Strasbourg: 45 min direct. Incredibly easy.

🛁 Baths — The Whole Point

▼
♨️
Friedrichsbad (Roman-Irish Bath)

The bucket list bathhouse. A 19th-century palatial spa built directly over the Roman springs. No swimwear — the 17-stage bathing ritual (hot rooms, warm rooms, brushdown massage, cool plunge, rest) takes about 3 hours. Mark Twain wrote about this place. Utterly unlike anything else. Tattoo policy: tattoos are accepted. No swimwear required or permitted in most areas.

♨️
Caracalla Spa

The modern complement to Friedrichsbad. Same thermal water, swimwear-friendly, indoor and outdoor pools, grottos, saunas, and a relaxation area. More accessible if Friedrichsbad's nudity requirement feels like a lot. Tattoo policy: welcoming.

♨️
Roman Bath ruins (Römerplatz)

The original Roman bathhouse beneath the modern city — partially excavated and open to visit. Puts the whole spa tradition in context. Free or very cheap.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Black Forest bakeries

Baden-Baden sits at the edge of the Schwarzwald. Bakeries here do Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte (Black Forest cake), dense rye breads, and cream-heavy pastries. The quality is high and the portions are serious.

🍽️
Restaurants near the Kurhaus

The elegant casino and Kurhaus district has excellent restaurants — German comfort food done well. Maultaschen (stuffed pasta), Flammekuchen (the German version of tarte flambée), and good local wine.

🥾 Hiking

▼
🌲
Black Forest trails from town

Trails start directly from the edge of Baden-Baden. The Merkur mountain (668m) is a 2-hour return hike with panoramic views — or take the funicular up and walk down. The Lichtentaler Allee riverside walk is flat, beautiful, and good for a morning warm-up.

⛰️
Merkur Mountain

The local peak above Baden-Baden. Funicular runs year-round. Walk up through beech forest, take the funicular down, stop at the spa on the way back. Classic Baden-Baden afternoon.

Salzburg
Wednesday–Friday, Days 7–9 🌙🌙🌙

Austria's most beautiful city — and crucially, its most manageable. Salzburg is compact, walkable, and set at the foot of the Alps with the Salzach river running through its centre. The Baroque old town is UNESCO-listed, the food markets are excellent, and the hiking starts immediately outside the city. A short train ride south takes you into the Alps and Bad Gastein — one of Europe's most dramatic thermal spa towns, set in a steep Alpine gorge with waterfalls frozen in January.

🚂 Train from Baden-Baden via Munich: ~3h. Comfortable and scenic.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
Austrian Bakery Culture
🥐
Kipferl and Semmel

Austrian breakfast bakeries are exceptional. Kipferl are crescent rolls (the croissant's ancestor), Semmel are soft white rolls with a distinctive six-pointed score. Get them warm from any Bäckerei before 9am. Eat with butter and apricot jam.

🍫
Mozartkugeln

Salzburg's famous chocolate — marzipan and nougat centre, dark chocolate shell. Invented here in 1890. The original Fürst version (in silver foil) is sold only from the original shop. Worth seeking out over the tourist versions.

🎂
Salzburger Nockerl

A soufflé-style dessert — three golden peaks of egg white and sugar, served tableside. The peaks represent the three hills above Salzburg. Theatrical, delicious, and very Austrian.

Markets
🛒
Green Market (Universitätsplatz)

Daily outdoor market in the heart of the old town. Local cheese, bread, sausage, and pastries. The most atmospheric place for breakfast in Salzburg — pick up provisions and eat standing.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
Hohensalzburg Fortress

The enormous medieval fortress above the city — one of the largest and best-preserved in Europe. Take the funicular up, walk down through the old town. The views over the Alps in January are stunning.

🏛️
Getreidegasse & Old Town

The main shopping street of the old town — lined with guild signs in wrought iron and buildings unchanged since Mozart's time. His birthplace is here. The whole street is a museum without an entrance fee.

🌿
Mirabell Palace & Gardens

Baroque palace with formal gardens. In January the gardens are quiet and frost-dusted but the palace interior is open and warm. The Sound of Music filmed the "Do-Re-Mi" sequence here.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Bad Gastein — Day Trip (⭐ Don't Skip)

One of Europe's most dramatic thermal spa experiences. Bad Gastein is a spa town built vertically into an Alpine gorge — Belle Époque hotels stacked up a steep hillside with a waterfall crashing down the centre. The thermal water is radon-rich and genuinely warm. The Felsentherme complex is the main bath facility. January means snow on the peaks, mist over the gorge, and the thermal pools steaming against frozen air. About 1.5h by train south of Salzburg. Tattoo policy: welcoming.

♨️
Paracelsusbad Salzburg

The city's own thermal bath complex. Outdoor and indoor pools, sauna, good for an evening session without the train trip. A solid backup if Bad Gastein day trip doesn't fit the schedule.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Untersberg Mountain

The iconic peak directly south of Salzburg. Cable car goes to 1776m — from there trails lead across the plateau with views into Bavaria and the Austrian Alps. In January bring proper gear — it's genuine Alpine terrain up top. The walk back down to the valley is about 3 hours.

🌊
Salzach River Walk

Flat, beautiful, and easy. Walk the riverside path south from the old town for an hour and you're already into farm country with Alpine views. A good morning warm-up before the fortress climb.

🏔️
Bad Gastein Gorge Walk

The waterfall gorge trail in Bad Gastein is hikeable year-round. The frozen spray on the cliff walls in January is extraordinary. Pair with a bath session after — you've earned it.

Essential Notes — Trip 1

💶 Currency

France and Germany use Euro. Austria uses Euro. One currency the whole trip — easy.

🌡️ January Weather

Strasbourg and Baden-Baden: 0–5°C, possibly frosty. Salzburg: -2 to 5°C, likely some snow. Bad Gastein: proper Alpine winter. Pack real layers, waterproof boots, and enjoy it — the baths hit differently when it's cold outside.

♨️ Friedrichsbad Booking

Book in advance at carasana.de — it fills up, especially weekends. The nudity aspect is non-negotiable; everyone is in the same situation and it's genuinely not awkward after 5 minutes.

🚂 Train Booking

France: SNCF Connect app. Germany: DB Navigator app. Austria: oebb.at. All three systems connect seamlessly — you can book cross-border tickets on DB Navigator for the whole Baden-Baden → Salzburg leg.

🥾 Hiking Gear

Microspikes or screw-in boot cleats are worth packing for January Alpine trails. Not always needed but invaluable if there's been recent snow. Pack layers that compress small.

Trip 2 — Alsace → Swiss Edge → Innsbruck

The mountain immersion. After Alsace's wine villages and bakeries, you cross into Switzerland briefly — Basel, Bern, or the Bernese Oberland edge — then drop into Austria via the Inn valley to Innsbruck. Innsbruck is smaller and more dramatic than Salzburg, ringed tightly by the Alps, and the Tyrol has some of the best thermal bath culture in Austria. Slower pace, more hiking, deeper into the mountains.

Strasbourg + Alsace Villages
Friday–Sunday, Days 2–4 🌙🌙🌙

Three days in Alsace. Strasbourg as your base for the first two nights, then a full day on the wine route. Same Alsace content as Trip 1 — cathedral, Petite France, bakeries, bouchons, Vosges hiking, thermal baths — but with an extra day to go deeper into the villages.

✈️ Arriving via Basel airport. 🚂 Basel → Strasbourg: 1h by train.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Kougelhopf

The Alsatian crown pastry — ring-shaped brioche with raisins, almonds, and powdered sugar. Every boulangerie. Eat warm, with butter.

🥨
Bretzel

The original soft pretzel, from Alsace. Lye crust, coarse salt, chewy interior. Nothing like stadium versions. Pick one up for the walk every morning.

🥗
Tarte flambée

Thin crispy dough, crème fraîche, lardons, onion. The Alsatian bouchon staple. Order multiple.

🗺️ Highlights

▼
⛪
Strasbourg Cathedral

Gothic masterpiece, 250 years in the building. Climb to the platform for Rhine valley views.

🏘️
La Petite France

Half-timbered canal district. Empty in January. Extremely beautiful.

🏘️
Wine Villages — Riquewihr, Eguisheim, Kaysersberg

Day trip south along the Route des Vins. Medieval walled villages with excellent pâtisseries. Rent a car for the day.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Les Bains Municipaux, Strasbourg

1908 Art Nouveau municipal baths. Hot pools, steam, locals-only vibe. Very affordable. The real bathhouse experience before the luxury versions in Austria.

♨️
Niederbronn-les-Bains

Roman-origin thermal springs, 40 min north of Strasbourg. Tiny spa town, completely genuine, almost no tourists in January.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Vosges Mountains

30 min west of Strasbourg by car. Forested ridge hiking with castle ruins on the summits. The GR5 long-distance trail runs the full length. Clear winter views into the Rhine plain.

Bern + Interlaken edge
Monday–Tuesday, Days 5–6 🌙🌙

Cross into Switzerland. Bern is the Swiss capital and one of Europe's best-preserved medieval cities — six kilometres of covered arcaded walkways line the old town streets, all UNESCO-listed. It's compact, walkable, and genuinely beautiful. From Bern, Interlaken is 50 minutes by train and gives access to the Bernese Oberland — the most dramatic Alpine scenery in Switzerland. January means snow-capped peaks, few tourists, and excellent bakeries.

🚂 Train from Strasbourg → Basel → Bern: about 2h total. Clean connection.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Gipfeli (Swiss croissant)

Switzerland's version of the croissant — slightly denser, more buttery, less flaky. Swiss bakeries (Bäckerei) open early and the Gipfeli situation is serious. The quality is exceptional across the board.

🧀
Fondue and Raclette

January in Switzerland is peak fondue season. Melted Gruyère or Emmental in a communal pot, bread dipped in — perfect after a cold morning of walking the arcades. Raclette (scraped melted cheese over potatoes) is the simpler weeknight version.

🐻
Bern markets (Bundesplatz)

Bern's outdoor markets operate year-round around the Federal Palace square. Local cheese, bread, and seasonal produce. Good for a slow morning browse.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏛️
Bern's UNESCO Arcades (Lauben)

Six kilometres of covered stone walkways running through the old town. Built in the 12th century to keep shoppers dry. The whole city centre is navigable under cover — remarkable urban design that still works perfectly.

🐻
Bear Park & Rosengarten viewpoint

Bern's heraldic animal is the bear — live bears live in a riverside enclosure below the old town. The Rosengarten above it has the best viewpoint over the Aare river bend and the Alps behind.

🏔️
Interlaken + Jungfrau views (day trip)

50 min by train from Bern. Interlaken sits between two lakes with the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau directly above. January means the peaks are white and the valley is quiet. You don't need to ski to enjoy this — just being there is enough.

🥾 Hiking

▼
🌊
Aare River Loop, Bern

Flat riverside walking path around the Aare river peninsula that the old town sits on. About 4km, beautiful even in winter, with views up to the old town walls. Good warm-up walk on arrival day.

🏔️
Harder Kulm above Interlaken

A ridge above Interlaken with views of all three main Bernese Alps peaks. Funicular goes up, trail comes down. In January bring traction devices — the descent can be icy. Worth it for the panorama.

Innsbruck
Wednesday–Friday, Days 7–9 🌙🌙🌙

The Tyrolean capital — smaller and more dramatically Alpine than Salzburg. Innsbruck sits in a narrow valley with 2000-metre peaks rising directly above the rooftops. The old town is compact and beautiful, the food market is excellent, and the surrounding Tyrol has some of Austria's best thermal bath villages. Wattens (just 15 min by train) has the Swarovski Crystal Worlds, an genuinely bizarre and wonderful underground art installation. The hiking starts immediately at the city's edge.

🚂 Train from Bern via Zürich: ~4h. The Arlberg route through the mountains is one of the great European train journeys.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Tyrolean Bäckereien

Austrian bakery culture is exceptional and Innsbruck has excellent ones. Kipferl (crescent rolls), Semmel (soft scored rolls), and Germknödel (sweet steamed dumplings filled with plum jam, dusted with poppy seed and butter). Get the Germknödel — it's absurdly good.

🥩
Tiroler Gröstl

The Tyrolean mountain dish — diced potatoes and beef pan-fried with onions and caraway, topped with a fried egg. Hearty, warming, and exactly what you want after a cold morning hike.

🛒
Markthalle Innsbruck

Innsbruck's covered food hall. Local cheese, bread, Tyrolean ham (Speck), fresh pasta, pastries. Open weekday mornings. The best breakfast in the city.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Aqua Dome, Längenfeld ⭐

One of the most architecturally striking thermal spas in the world. Three futuristic bowl-shaped outdoor pools perched above an Alpine valley, filled with thermal water, steam rising into the mountain air. About 1h from Innsbruck by bus or car. Tattoo policy: welcoming. Swimwear required in main pools, textile-free sauna area available.

♨️
Stubaital Thermal Baths (Stubay)

30 min south of Innsbruck by bus. The Stubaital valley has a well-regarded thermal complex combined with a ski area — but the baths are open to non-skiers. Good option if Aqua Dome feels far.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Nordkette — Cable Car + Ridge Walk

The cable car from the city centre takes you to 2256m in 20 minutes. From the top, the ridge walk across the Hafelekar gives views over Innsbruck, the Inn Valley, and the Stubai Alps. In January this is proper Alpine terrain — crampons or microspikes required above the cable car station. Extraordinary.

🌊
Inn River Path

Flat riverside walking and cycling path along the Inn. Easy, beautiful, good morning warm-up. The view back toward the old town with the mountains behind it is the postcard shot of Innsbruck.

Essential Notes — Trip 2

💶💷 Currency

France and Austria use Euro. Switzerland uses Swiss Franc (CHF) — budget roughly 20–30% more for Swiss days as prices are higher than France or Austria.

🏔️ Alpine Safety in January

The Nordkette above Innsbruck and Harder Kulm above Interlaken are real Alpine environments in January. Check conditions before going up, bring microspikes, and don't push past your comfort level. The views are worth the preparation.

♨️ Aqua Dome Booking

Book the Aqua Dome in advance at aqua-dome.at — it's popular even in winter. The bus connection from Innsbruck is called the Ötztal bus and runs regularly.

🚂 Switzerland Rail

Swiss trains are famously punctual and comfortable. The SBB app handles all Swiss train tickets. The Bern → Innsbruck route via Zürich and the Arlberg tunnel is one of the great European train journeys — get a window seat.

Trip 3 — Lyon + Annecy + Salzburg

The food capital route. Fly into Lyon — France's undisputed culinary capital, home of the bouchon tradition and the country's best street-level food culture. Then east to Annecy, a town so beautiful it looks painted, on the clearest lake in Europe, ringed by Alps. Then Salzburg for the Austrian finale — architecture, markets, and a day trip to Bad Gastein's thermal gorge. The most food-focused of the three options, with equally strong hiking and bath culture.

Thursday, Day 1 — Portland → Lyon

PDX to Lyon (LYS) via Paris CDG. Air France flies PDX → CDG, then a short connection to Lyon. Alternatively fly into Paris and take the TGV to Lyon — 2 hours, runs constantly. Arrive Day 2 morning.

✈️ PDX → CDG → LYS. Air France or Delta through Paris is the cleanest option.
Lyon
Friday–Sunday, Days 2–4 🌙🌙🌙

France's real food capital. Paris is glamorous but Lyon is where the serious eating happens — the bouchon tradition (working-class bistros serving rich, unfussy food) was invented here, and the city has more great restaurants per capita than anywhere in France. Paul Bocuse built his empire here. The old town (Vieux-Lyon) is a Renaissance quarter of narrow traboules (hidden passageways) and stone courtyards. Three days is not too many.

✈️ Arriving via Paris CDG and connection, or TGV from Paris.

🥐 Bakeries & Food — Lyon Is The Point

▼
The Bouchon Tradition
🍽️
Bouchons Lyonnais

The bouchon is Lyon's soul. Small, warm bistros serving traditional working-class Lyonnais cooking — quenelles de brochet (pike dumplings in cream sauce), salade lyonnaise (frisée with lardons and a poached egg), andouillette sausage, coq au vin. Certified bouchons display an official plaque. Lunch is the main meal.

🧀
Les Halles Paul Bocuse

Lyon's covered food market — named after the city's most famous chef. 60 stalls of exceptional cheese, charcuterie, pastry, fish, and produce. Go on a weekday morning. This is the best food market in France.

Bakeries
🥐
Tarte à la praline (pink praline tart)

The Lyonnais pastry signature. A shortcrust shell filled with crushed pink pralines that melt into a caramelised filling. Bright pink, slightly alarming, utterly delicious. Every boulangerie in Lyon makes one.

🍩
Bugnes

Lyon's fried dough pastry — thin, crispy, dusted with powdered sugar. A January / pre-Mardi-Gras tradition in Lyon. Light and addictive. Pick up a bag at any bakery.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏘️
Vieux-Lyon & Traboules

The Renaissance old town is UNESCO-listed and remarkable. The traboules are hidden passageways cutting through buildings from street to courtyard to street — a secret network built by silk merchants. Many are open to walk through. Allow half a day to explore properly.

⛪
Fourvière Basilica & Roman Theatres

The hill above the old town has a 19th-century basilica and two well-preserved Roman theatres from the 1st century BC. The views over Lyon from the esplanade are exceptional in clear January weather.

🏛️
Presqu'île (the peninsula)

The heart of modern Lyon — a long peninsula between two rivers. The Place des Terreaux, the covered passages (like Alsatian arcades but grander), the best restaurant streets, and the main shopping area. Easy walking.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Hammam & Spa options, Lyon

Lyon has several well-regarded hammam and spa options in the city. Not a thermal town per se, but the hammam tradition is strong. Good for an evening session after a heavy bouchon lunch. The serious thermal baths come later in the trip.

Annecy
Monday–Tuesday, Days 5–6 🌙🌙

Often called the Venice of the Alps — which sounds like tourist copy but is genuinely accurate. Annecy is built around canals feeding into the clearest, cleanest lake in Europe, with the Alps rising sharply on three sides. The old town is small and beautiful, the lake path is one of the great easy walks in France, and the mountains above offer serious hiking. In January the crowds are completely gone, the lake is glassy and cold, and the surrounding peaks are snow-covered. Exceptional bakeries throughout.

🚂 Train from Lyon: 2h. Simple direct service.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Savoyard pastries and bread

Annecy is in the Savoie region — the Alpine bread and pastry tradition here is distinct from the rest of France. Dense rye breads, Saint-Genix brioche (a brioche studded with pink pralines — the Savoyard version of the Lyonnais tart), and excellent croissants everywhere.

🧀
Savoyard cheese — Reblochon & Beaufort

The Savoie is cheese country. Reblochon (the cheese in tartiflette), Beaufort (a firm Alpine cheese aged in mountain caves), Tomme de Savoie. The Tuesday and Friday markets in Annecy are outstanding for cheese shopping.

🥔
Tartiflette

The Alpine comfort dish — potatoes, Reblochon cheese, lardons, and onions baked until bubbling. The perfect meal after a cold lake walk. Every restaurant in Annecy does it.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
Palais de l'Isle

A 12th-century prison building sitting on a triangular island in the middle of the main canal. The most-photographed building in Annecy. Genuinely striking from every angle — especially reflected in the still winter canal water.

🏰
Château d'Annecy

The hilltop castle above the old town — part museum, part viewpoint over the lake and Alps. The view from the terrace is exactly the image you have in your head when someone says "Annecy."

🌊
Lake Path (Voie Verte)

A flat, paved path running along the entire lake edge. In January the light on the water and the snow-capped mountains behind is extraordinary. Easy walking or cycling — bike hire available year-round.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Centre Aquatique d'Annecy

Annecy's main aquatic and spa centre. Heated pools, sauna, and steam rooms with mountain views. More municipal than luxury but very well run. Good for a recovery session after a hike.

♨️
Aix-les-Bains (40 min, serious thermal town)

A proper Roman-origin thermal spa town 40 minutes from Annecy. The Thermes Nationaux d'Aix-les-Bains has been in continuous operation since the Romans — sulfurous springs, proper spa facilities, and a beautiful lakeside setting. Worth the detour.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Mont Veyrier

A 2–3 hour return hike directly from town, up through forest to a ridge with spectacular views over Lac d'Annecy. The lake laid out below with the Alps behind it in January snow is genuinely one of the great views in the Alps. Bring microspikes if there's been recent snow.

🌊
Lake Loop Walk

The flat path around the lake is about 35km for the full loop — but you can walk any section and turn back. A morning walk south from town along the eastern shore, 10km return, is beautiful and easy.

Salzburg
Wednesday–Friday, Days 7–9 🌙🌙🌙

The Austrian finale. Compact Baroque city on the Salzach river, fortress on the hill, the Alps immediately behind. Salzburg has excellent food markets, world-class patisseries, and is the gateway to Bad Gastein — one of Europe's most dramatic and beautiful thermal spa experiences, a Belle Époque spa town built into a steep Alpine gorge. Three days here leaves room for a full Bad Gastein day and still time to explore the old town properly.

🚂 Train from Annecy via Geneva and Zürich: ~6h. A spectacular Alpine crossing.

🥐 Bakeries & Food

▼
🥐
Kipferl, Semmel, and Germknödel

Austrian bakery culture is quietly exceptional. Kipferl (crescent rolls, the original croissant), Semmel (soft rolls), and Germknödel (steamed dumplings filled with plum jam and smothered in poppy seed butter) are the morning essentials.

🍫
Original Mozartkugeln — Fürst

The original 1890 recipe — marzipan and pistachio nougat in dark chocolate, silver foil. Only sold at the original Fürst shop. Completely different from the mass-produced versions in gold foil at tourist shops.

🛒
Green Market (Universitätsplatz)

Daily outdoor market in the old town. Local cheese, bread, charcuterie, and pastries from regional producers. Best breakfast in Salzburg — eat standing at the stalls.

🛁 Baths — Bad Gastein Day Trip ⭐

▼
♨️
Bad Gastein

Don't miss this. Bad Gastein is a Victorian and Belle Époque spa town stacked vertically up the walls of an Alpine gorge, with a waterfall crashing down its centre. The radon-rich thermal water is genuinely therapeutic. The Felsentherme is the main bath complex — outdoor and indoor pools with the gorge walls and waterfall directly visible. In January: snow on the peaks, steam rising from the pools, the frozen spray on the cliff walls catching the light. Magical. 1.5h south of Salzburg by direct train. Tattoo policy: welcoming.

♨️
Paracelsusbad, Salzburg

Salzburg's own thermal pool complex. Good for an evening session when you don't want to travel. Indoor and outdoor pools, sauna, solid relaxation facilities.

🥾 Hiking

▼
⛰️
Untersberg Mountain

Cable car to 1776m, then trails across the Alpine plateau. Views into Bavaria and across the Austrian Alps. Proper January Alpine terrain — crampons needed on the plateau. The descent through the forest is gentler.

🌊
Bad Gastein Gorge Walk

The waterfall gorge trail is hikeable year-round. The frozen spray on the cliff walls in January makes it extraordinary. Pair the walk with a Felsentherme session — hike in, soak out.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
Hohensalzburg Fortress

One of Europe's largest and best-preserved medieval fortresses. Take the funicular up, walk down through the old town. Views over the Alps in winter are outstanding.

🏛️
Getreidegasse & Mozart's Birthplace

The main old town shopping street — unchanged since Mozart's time, lined with wrought-iron guild signs. His birthplace is here. The street itself is a museum without an entrance fee.

Essential Notes — Trip 3

🍽️ Lyon Bouchon Tips

Certified bouchons display an official "Authentique Bouchon Lyonnais" plaque. Lunch is the main meal — dinner service is shorter and some bouchons close early. Book ahead even in January for the popular ones.

💶 Currency

France and Austria both use Euro. One currency the entire trip.

🏔️ The Annecy → Salzburg Train

The route via Geneva and Zürich takes about 6 hours but passes through some extraordinary Alpine scenery — Lac Léman, the Swiss midlands, and the Salzburg approach through the Austrian Alps. Get a window seat and don't sleep through it.

♨️ Bad Gastein — Book the Train

The ÖBB direct train from Salzburg to Bad Gastein runs several times daily. Book on oebb.at in advance. The town itself is tiny — one main street of thermal facilities, cafes, and that extraordinary gorge.

🥾 January Hiking Gear

Microspikes for Annecy's Mont Veyrier and anything above 1500m in Austria. Waterproof boots throughout. Layers that compress. The cold is genuinely pleasant when you're dressed for it.
