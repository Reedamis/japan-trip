Trip 1 — Andalusia + Alsace, France

Maximum contrast. Moorish palaces and sherry tapas in southern Spain, then fairy-tale half-timbered wine villages and WWI memorial sites in northeastern France. Two completely different worlds, both deeply food-obsessed and walkable on a human scale. Best fit if WWI history is a priority.

Fly in: PDX → Madrid → Málaga (AGP)
Fly out: Strasbourg (SXB) or Basel (BSL) → PDX
Mid-trip flight: Málaga → Strasbourg (via Madrid or Frankfurt)
Ground transport: Train + walking in both regions
✈️ Travel Day
Thursday, Day 1 — Portland → Málaga

PDX to Málaga with one connection, typically through Madrid (Iberia) or Amsterdam/Frankfurt (KLM/Lufthansa). Total travel time ~16–20 hours. Arrive Day 2 morning local time.

🚆 Tip: Book PDX → MAD → AGP on Iberia for the smoothest single-ticket connection.
Málaga
Friday–Saturday, Days 2–3 🌙🌙

Your soft landing. Málaga is a port city with a genuinely great food scene, a walkable old town, and a warm January climate (~15°C). It punches above its weight for a mid-size city — Roman theatre, Moorish Alcazaba, Picasso's birthplace, and some of the best breakfast bars in Spain. Not intimidating at all.

✈️ Arriving by plane from PDX

🍜 Food

▼
Breakfast
☕
Café con leche + tostada con tomate

The Andalusian breakfast ritual. Any bar in the old town will do it well — toasted bread rubbed with tomato and olive oil. Cheap, essential, perfect.

Lunch / Dinner
🐟
Espetos de sardinas

Málaga's signature dish — whole sardines skewered on cane rods and grilled over a wood fire on the beach. Unmissable in January when crowds are gone.

🍷
Tapas bars around El Pimpi district

The old bodega district. Order fino sherry alongside small plates. Boquerones (anchovies), gambas al ajillo, and salmorejo are local staples.

Bakeries & Snacks
🥐
Pastelerías in the old town

Look for polvorones (crumbly almond shortbread) and roscos de vino (anise wine biscuits) — both peak season in January as post-Christmas treats.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
Alcazaba of Málaga

11th-century Moorish fortress with exceptional city and harbour views. Built from Roman ruins, so you're looking at two civilisations at once. Free on Sundays.

🏛️
Roman Theatre

1st century BC, right at the base of the Alcazaba. Free to visit and genuinely impressive for a city most people underestimate.

🎨
Picasso Museum

Málaga was Picasso's birthplace. The museum is small and very good. January means no queues.

🚶
Cathedral & Historic Centre walk

The cathedral is nicknamed "La Manquita" (the one-armed lady) because one tower was never finished. The surrounding streets are excellent for wandering.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Hammam Al Ándalus

Arab bath complex in a beautifully restored 16th-century building. Hot and cold pools, steam room, optional massage. Tattoo policy: covered tattoos are fine, visible tattoos generally accepted. Book ahead.

🚆 Transit & Getting Around

▼
WalkThe historic centre is entirely walkable. Airport to city: taxi (~€20) or metro (Line 1, ~€1.80).

Ronda
Sunday, Day 4 🌙 — Day Trip from Málaga

One of the most dramatic towns in Spain, full stop. Ronda sits on a sheer 100-metre gorge and is connected by a single 18th-century stone bridge. Tiny, walkable, genuinely breathtaking. The drive or bus ride through the mountains is half the experience. Come for the day, or stay a night if you want peace and quiet.

🚌 Bus from Málaga bus station, ~2 hours. Or rent a car for the mountain scenery.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🌉
Puente Nuevo (New Bridge)

The iconic 18th-century bridge spanning the 120-metre El Tajo gorge. The views from the bridge and the path below it are extraordinary. Free to walk across.

🏟️
Plaza de Toros

One of the oldest and most beautiful bullrings in Spain (1785). Even if bullfighting isn't your thing, the ring and its museum are architectural and cultural history worth seeing.

🏘️
La Ciudad (Old Moorish Quarter)

The southern half of Ronda dates to Moorish rule. Narrow whitewashed streets, a 13th-century baths ruin, and quiet plazas. Takes about 2 hours to wander properly.

🍜 Food

▼
🍖
Rabo de toro (oxtail stew)

The regional signature dish. Rich, slow-braised, deeply savoury. Every restaurant in Ronda does a version — it's what you eat here.

🫙
Local olive oil and honey shops

The Serranía de Ronda produces excellent olive oil and wildflower honey. Pick some up to carry home — both pack well.

Granada
Monday–Tuesday, Days 5–6 🌙🌙

The crown of Andalusia. Granada has the Alhambra — the greatest surviving Moorish palace complex in the world — and one of Spain's best tapas traditions (free tapas with every drink, still, in Granada). The Albaicín neighbourhood is a UNESCO-listed Moorish quarter of steep whitewashed lanes and tea houses. January is cold but crowd-free.

🚌 Bus from Málaga, ~1.5–2 hours. Direct and frequent.

🍜 Food

▼
The Granada Tapas Rule
🍺
Free tapas with every drink

Unlike the rest of Spain, Granada still brings you a free tapa with each beer or wine. Bars around Plaza Nueva and Calle Navas are the best hunting ground. Order a few rounds and you have a meal.

🫕
Plato alpujarreño

Mountain-style plate: potatoes, blood sausage, chorizo, egg. Hearty and warming for a cold January in the Sierra Nevada foothills.

Tea Houses (Teterías)
🍵
Moroccan-style tea houses, Albaicín

The Albaicín has dozens of beautiful Moroccan-influenced tea rooms — cushioned floors, mint tea, pastries. Perfect afternoon stop after climbing the hill.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
The Alhambra

Book tickets weeks in advance — entry is strictly timed and sells out even in January. The Nasrid Palaces are the highlight: intricate tilework, carved plaster, reflecting pools. Give it a full half-day minimum.

🕌
Albaicín Quarter

The Moorish hilltop neighbourhood across the gorge from the Alhambra. Wander up through narrow carmenes (walled garden houses) to the Mirador de San Nicolás for the famous Alhambra view at sunset.

⛪
Cathedral & Royal Chapel

Where Ferdinand and Isabella are buried. The Royal Chapel is small, quiet, and genuinely moving — two of history's most consequential monarchs in a plain stone tomb.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Hammam Al Ándalus Granada

The Granada outpost of the same chain as Málaga — stunning vaulted ceilings, hot and cold pools, candlelit ambiance. Sits in the Albaicín. Tattoo policy: generally welcoming, swimwear required. Book ahead, evenings fill up.

🚆 Transit & Essentials

▼
BusGranada to Málaga: ALSA bus, ~1h45. Frequent. Book online.
WalkAlbaicín and city centre are walkable. The hill is steep — wear good shoes.
Alhambra Booking — Critical
⚠️
Book Alhambra tickets before you leave home

Tickets sell out weeks in advance even in winter. Official site: alhambra-patronato.es. Timed entry for the Nasrid Palaces is separate from general grounds.

✈️ Transit Day
Wednesday, Day 7 — Granada/Málaga → Strasbourg

Train or bus back to Málaga, then fly to Strasbourg via Madrid or Frankfurt. Strasbourg airport (SXB) is small and easy. Arrive early evening — pick up provisions and settle in.

✈️ Fly: AGP → MAD → SXB or AGP → FRA → SXB. Budget ~€100–180 for this leg.
Strasbourg
Thursday–Friday, Days 8–9 🌙🌙

Your base for Alsace. Strasbourg is the capital of the region — a genuinely beautiful city of canals, half-timbered houses, and a towering Gothic cathedral. It's the EU Parliament city, but feels nothing like a bureaucratic capital. The old town (Grande Île) is an island surrounded by canals and is UNESCO-listed. January is cold and quiet, with warm bouchons (traditional restaurants) everywhere.

✈️ Arriving by flight from Málaga

🍜 Food

▼
Alsatian Specialties
🥗
Tarte flambée (Flammekueche)

Alsace's answer to pizza — thin crispy dough with crème fraîche, lardons, and onions. Found in every bouchon. Order multiple, they're small and addictive.

🥬
Choucroute garnie

Alsatian sauerkraut with pork sausage, knuckle, and potatoes. A serious winter dish. The kind of meal that requires a nap afterward.

Bakeries
🥨
Bretzels and kougelhopf

Alsace invented the pretzel (bretzel here). Kougelhopf is the regional sweet bread — a ring-shaped brioche with raisins and almonds. Find both in any boulangerie.

Wine
🍷
Alsatian Riesling & Gewurztraminer

Alsace produces some of France's best whites. Order by the glass in any bouchon — Riesling is crisp and mineral, Gewurztraminer is aromatic and slightly sweet.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
⛪
Strasbourg Cathedral (Notre-Dame)

One of the tallest medieval buildings in the world. The Gothic facade is extraordinarily detailed — took 250 years to build. Climb to the platform for panoramic views of the Rhine plain.

🏘️
La Petite France

The most photographed corner of Strasbourg — half-timbered houses reflected in the canal, lock gates, weir walkways. Genuinely as pretty as the photos. Empty in January.

🖼️
Musée Alsacien

Folk museum inside three connected 17th-century houses. Covers Alsatian rural life, craft traditions, and religious objects. Small, warm, and deeply regional.

⚔️ WWI History — Day Trip

▼
🪖
Hartmannswillerkopf (Vieil Armand)

WWI memorial site. A mountain ridge south of Colmar where French and German forces fought brutal battles in 1914–1915. National monument with ossuary, trenches, and memorial museum. Deeply moving. ~1 hour from Strasbourg by car.

🪦
WWI cemeteries of Alsace

The Alsatian front saw some of the war's earliest fighting. Scattered German and French military cemeteries dot the Vosges foothills — most are free, quiet, and accessible by car. Neuf-Brisach and the Rhine fortifications are also nearby.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Les Bains Municipaux

Strasbourg's historic municipal baths — an Art Nouveau building from 1908 with thermal pools. Locals use it regularly. Very affordable, very genuine. Tattoo policy: generally relaxed.

♨️
Niederbronn-les-Bains (Day Trip)

A small spa town 40 minutes north of Strasbourg with Roman-origin thermal springs. The whole town is built around the baths. Charming, tiny, and exactly the kind of quiet place you described wanting.

▼

Alsace Wine Villages
Friday, Day 9 — Day Trips from Strasbourg

The Route des Vins d'Alsace (Alsace Wine Route) runs south from Strasbourg through some of the most absurdly picturesque small towns in France. These are the places that look like they're from a fairy tale — because they basically are. All within 1 hour of Strasbourg by train or car.

🚗 Best by rental car or taxi. Colmar is also easy by train (30 min).

🗺️ Villages Worth Visiting

▼
🏘️
Colmar

The main hub of the wine route. A full city of half-timbered houses, canals (the "Little Venice" district), excellent museums, and great restaurants. Worth 3–4 hours at minimum.

🏘️
Riquewihr

A tiny walled village almost entirely unchanged since the 16th century. No cars inside the walls. Lined with wine shops and bakeries. January means almost no tourists — it's yours.

🏘️
Eguisheim

A circular medieval village built in concentric rings around a castle courtyard. Voted one of France's most beautiful villages. Geranium-lined in summer, frost-quiet in January.

🏘️
Kaysersberg

Albert Schweitzer's birthplace. A village with a ruined castle, a fortified bridge, and some of the best pâtisseries on the wine route. A perfect lunch stop.

Saturday, Day 10 — Strasbourg/Basel → Portland

Fly from Strasbourg (SXB) or Basel (BSL — 1h by train) back to PDX, typically via Paris, Frankfurt, or Amsterdam. Basel EuroAirport actually serves both Switzerland and France and often has better options.

Essential Notes — Trip 1

💶 Currency

Spain and France both use Euro. Carry some cash for markets and small village bakeries — card acceptance varies outside cities.

🌡️ January Weather

Málaga and Granada: 10–15°C, sunny days likely. Strasbourg and Alsace: 0–5°C, possibly frosty or light snow. Pack layers — the contrast is real.

🏰 Alhambra Tickets

Book before you leave Portland. Sell out weeks in advance even in low season. alhambra-patronato.es — timed entry, non-refundable.

📱 Transport Apps

Spain: ALSA (buses), Renfe (trains). France: SNCF Connect app for trains. For Alsace day trips, consider renting a car for one day via Europcar or Sixt from Strasbourg station.

✈️ Mid-Trip Flight

Book Málaga → Strasbourg as a separate ticket from your transatlantic flights. Vueling, Iberia, and Air France typically serve this route with one connection.

Trip 2 — Andalusia + Basque Country + Dordogne, France

The food pilgrim route. You move north through Spain by train — Málaga to Granada, then up to San Sebastián (the pintxos capital of the world), cross the border into southwestern France and disappear into the medieval villages of the Dordogne. Truffle country, foie gras, prehistoric caves, and rivers. Fly home from Bordeaux. Least flying of the three options after you land.

Fly in: PDX → Madrid → Málaga (AGP)
Fly out: Bordeaux (BOD) → PDX
Mid-trip: All train or bus — no internal flights needed
Ground transport: Train-heavy with one car rental in Dordogne

Thursday, Day 1 — Portland → Málaga

PDX to Málaga via Madrid or Amsterdam. Arrive morning of Day 2. The Iberia connection through Madrid is typically the smoothest and fastest option.

Málaga

Your arrival city and Andalusian warm-up. Beach city with a genuinely great food scene, a walkable Moorish fortress, Roman ruins, and excellent breakfast culture. Mild in January. Take it slow — you're recovering from the overnight flight.

Ronda
Sunday, Day 4 — Day Trip from Málaga

A cliffside town perched 750 metres above a sheer gorge, connected to itself by an 18th-century stone bridge. One of Spain's most dramatic small towns. A perfect day trip before heading north.

🚌 Bus from Málaga, ~2 hours. Or scenic drive through Sierra Nevada foothills.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🌉
Puente Nuevo & El Tajo Gorge

The 120-metre gorge and the 18th-century bridge spanning it. Walk the path down into the gorge for the underside view. Vertigo-inducing in the best way.

🏘️
La Ciudad (Old Town)

Moorish quarter with Arab baths ruins, narrow streets, and viewpoints over the Serranía. Allow 2–3 hours to wander properly.

🍜 Food

▼
🍖
Rabo de toro (oxtail stew)

The mountain signature — slow-braised oxtail, rich and warming. Every restaurant in Ronda does it well.

🚂 Heading North
Monday, Day 5 — Málaga → San Sebastián

A long but scenic train day. Málaga to Madrid on the AVE (high-speed, ~2h30), then Madrid to San Sebastián by train (~5h30). Arrive late afternoon. This is the moment the trip shifts — from Moorish south to Basque north.

🚂 Train: Renfe AVE Málaga→Madrid + Renfe San Sebastián→Donostia. Book ahead on renfe.com
San Sebastián (Donostia)
Monday–Wednesday, Days 5–7 🌙🌙🌙

Arguably the best food city in the world per capita. San Sebastián sits on a bay in the Basque Country — a crescent beach, a compact old town called Parte Vieja, and more Michelin stars per square metre than almost anywhere on earth. The pintxos bars of Parte Vieja are the main event: stand-up eating at the counter, tiny bites of extraordinary things, glass of txakoli wine in hand. Cold and dramatic in January.

🚂 Arriving by train from Madrid

🍜 Food

▼
The Pintxos Crawl (this is the whole point)
🍢
Pintxos bars, Parte Vieja

The old town has ~30 bars in a few blocks. The ritual: walk in, order one pintxo and a glass of txakoli (sparkling Basque white wine), eat standing at the bar, move to the next place. Do this for 2–3 hours. It is one of the great eating experiences in Europe.

🐚
Bacalao al pil-pil & grilled squid

Basque cooking is obsessed with salt cod and seafood. Pil-pil is a sauce made by emulsifying the fish's own gelatin with olive oil. Extraordinary.

Bakeries
🍮
Pastel vasco (Gâteau basque)

The Basque pastry — a shortcrust shell filled with pastry cream or cherry jam. Every pâtisserie makes one. Get it warm if you can.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏖️
La Concha Beach

The iconic curved bay. January is too cold to swim but the promenade walk is spectacular — wide, elegant, and almost empty. The city looks completely different from the water's edge.

⛰️
Monte Urgull

The wooded hill above the old town with a castle and Christ statue at the top. Free to walk up. Views of the bay, the beach, and the whole city. Takes about 40 minutes from base to summit.

🎨
San Telmo Museum

Basque culture and contemporary art in a converted 16th-century convent. Excellent context for understanding the Basque identity — which is genuinely distinct from Spanish identity in fascinating ways.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
La Perla Thalassotherapy Centre

Seawater spa right on La Concha beach. Pools fed with Atlantic seawater, steam rooms, jet showers. Very San Sebastián — serious wellness without pretension. Tattoo policy: welcoming. Swimwear required.

🚆 Transit & Essentials

▼
WalkParte Vieja and the beach are all walkable. The whole city is compact.
TrainSan Sebastián to Bayonne (France): EuskoTren or SNCF, ~40 minutes across the border.

Dordogne / Périgord, France
Thursday–Friday, Days 8–9 🌙🌙

Cross into southwestern France and enter one of the most beautiful and undervisited regions in the country. The Dordogne river valley is lined with medieval cliff-top castles, limestone villages, prehistoric cave art, and the richest food culture in France — truffle country, foie gras, walnut oil, confit de canard. In January you'll have the villages almost entirely to yourself. Base yourself in Sarlat-la-Canéda.

🚂 Train: San Sebastián → Bayonne → Bordeaux → Sarlat (via Périgueux). Or rent a car from Bordeaux — strongly recommended for the valley villages.

🍜 Food

▼
Périgord Classics
🦆
Confit de canard

Duck leg slow-cooked in its own fat — the canonical dish of the region. Every restaurant does it, none do it badly here. It is the comfort food of the Dordogne.

🍄
Black truffle (Périgord noir)

January is peak truffle season. The Dordogne's black truffles are among the most prized in the world. Sarlat and Périgueux have winter truffle markets — you can buy direct from producers. Scrambled eggs with truffle shaved over them is a local obsession.

🫙
Foie gras markets

The Dordogne is the source of most of France's foie gras. Local markets sell it fresh, whole, in terrine. Sarlat's Saturday market is one of the best in France even in January.

Bakeries
🥐
Walnut bread and gâteau aux noix

The Périgord grows excellent walnuts. Walnut oil, walnut bread, and dense walnut cake show up in every boulangerie and market stall.

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏘️
Sarlat-la-Canéda

The best-preserved medieval town in France. The entire centre is 14th and 15th-century stone — no fake renovations, no modern intrusions. Quiet and golden in January. The Saturday market is outstanding.

🪨
Lascaux IV (Prehistoric Cave Art)

The original Lascaux cave (17,000-year-old paintings) is sealed for preservation, but Lascaux IV is a complete full-scale replica with an excellent museum. One of the most important prehistoric art sites in the world. Near Montignac, ~30 min from Sarlat.

🏰
Castles of the Dordogne Valley

Beynac and Castelnaud face each other across the river — English vs French castles from the Hundred Years War. Both dramatically perched on limestone cliffs above the river. Drive the valley road between them.

🏘️
La Roque-Gageac

A village built directly into a limestone cliff above the river. Consistently voted one of France's most beautiful villages. Takes 20 minutes to walk the whole thing. Extraordinary.

🚆 Transit & Essentials

▼
CarA rental car is strongly recommended for the Dordogne. Castles and villages are spread across the valley — public transport is sparse in January.

Essential Notes — Trip 2

🍄 January is Truffle Season

The Périgord Noir truffle season peaks in January. Check market dates in Sarlat, Périgueux, and Sorges before you go — some markets are weekly, some are annual.

🚗 Rent a Car in the Dordogne

The valley villages are not accessible by public transport in winter. Rent from Bordeaux or Sarlat for your Dordogne days. Return at Bordeaux airport on departure.

🌡️ Weather Contrast

Málaga: mild (~14°C). San Sebastián: cold and rainy (~9°C, Atlantic weather). Dordogne: cold and possibly frosty (~5°C). Pack real layers for the French leg.

🚂 Train Booking

Spain: renfe.com for AVE (high-speed). France: SNCF Connect app. Book the San Sebastián → Bordeaux leg early — it's a popular cross-border route.

💶 Currency

Both Spain and France use Euro. The Dordogne markets are cash-heavy — bring enough for market purchases, especially for truffle and foie gras producers.

Trip 3 — Andalusia + Provence, France

The slow south play. Stay in Mediterranean warmth the whole trip. Moorish Andalusia gives way to Roman Provence — lavender fields (dormant but beautiful in winter), ochre cliff villages, Roman aqueducts, and extraordinary markets. All train travel once you land. Fly home from Marseille or Lyon. Least stressful of the three options, most cohesive mood.

Fly in: PDX → Madrid → Málaga (AGP)
Fly out: Marseille (MRS) or Lyon (LYS) → PDX
Mid-trip: Train from Málaga/Granada → Barcelona → Avignon/Marseille
Ground transport: Train-heavy + one car rental for Luberon villages

Ronda + Granada
Sunday–Tuesday, Days 4–6 🌙🌙🌙

Day trip to Ronda on Sunday (cliff-top gorge town, one of Spain's most dramatic), then move to Granada for Monday and Tuesday. The Alhambra and the Albaicín quarter are the heart of Andalusian Moorish history — and the free-tapas tradition means eating well is cheap.

🚌 Bus: Málaga→Ronda→Málaga (day trip). Then bus or train Málaga→Granada (1.5–2h).

🗺️ Highlights

▼
🌉
Ronda: Puente Nuevo & El Tajo Gorge

18th-century bridge across a 120-metre gorge. Walk the path down into the gorge for the view from below. One of the most dramatic settings in Spain.

🏰
Granada: The Alhambra

The greatest surviving Moorish palace complex in the world. Book timed-entry tickets before leaving Portland — sell out weeks ahead even in January.

🕌
Granada: Albaicín & Mirador de San Nicolás

The Moorish hilltop quarter. Wind up through narrow whitewashed streets to the viewpoint for the iconic Alhambra-at-sunset view. Cold in January but unforgettable.

♨️
Granada: Hammam Al Ándalus

Arab baths in the Albaicín — vaulted ceilings, candlelit pools, hot/cold circuit. A perfect evening after a long day of walking hills.

🍜 Food

▼
🍺
Granada's free tapas tradition

A free tapa comes with every drink in Granada. Wander Calle Navas and Plaza Nueva, order a beer or wine at each bar, eat what arrives. Two hours of this is a full meal.

🍵
Albaicín tea houses (teterías)

Moroccan-style tea rooms with mint tea, pastries, and cushioned floors. Perfect afternoon stop halfway up the hill.

🚂 Crossing to Provence
Wednesday, Day 7 — Granada → Barcelona → Avignon

A travel day, but a scenic one. Train from Granada to Barcelona (beautiful Mediterranean coast route), then TGV to Avignon. Arrive early evening — Avignon's old town is compact and walkable immediately on arrival.

🚂 Train: Granada→Barcelona (Renfe, ~5.5h) + Barcelona→Avignon TGV (~3h). Book ahead on renfe.com and SNCF.
Avignon
Thursday, Day 8 🌙

A walled medieval city on the Rhône — the seat of the Catholic papacy in the 14th century when the popes fled Rome. The Palais des Papes is one of the largest Gothic buildings in Europe. The city fits entirely inside its medieval walls, which you can still walk. January is cold but uncrowded and atmospheric.

🚂 Arriving by TGV from Barcelona

🗺️ Attractions

▼
🏰
Palais des Papes

The immense Gothic palace where seven popes lived in the 14th century. Austere, enormous, and unlike any building in France. An audio guide is essential — the empty rooms need context to come alive.

🌉
Pont Saint-Bénézet (the broken bridge)

The famous medieval bridge — half of it fell into the Rhône in the 17th century and was never repaired. The ruin is beautiful and there's a song about it every French child knows.

🚶
Walk the medieval ramparts

Avignon's intact city walls are 4.3km around. Walking the exterior perimeter takes about an hour and gives a good sense of the city's scale — rare to find a medieval enceinte this complete.

🍜 Food

▼
🫒
Provençal market food

Avignon's market (Les Halles) is covered and open daily. Tapenade, fresh chevre, Cavaillon melon jam, lavender honey, olives in dozens of varieties. Go before breakfast.

🥩
Daube Provençale

Provençal beef stew slow-cooked in red wine with olives, orange peel, and herbes de Provence. The definitive winter dish of the region.

Luberon Villages
Friday, Day 9 — Day Trip from Avignon

The Luberon massif southeast of Avignon contains some of France's most spectacular perched villages — hilltop stone towns with views across lavender fields (bare in January but hauntingly beautiful). A car is essential for this day.

🚗 Rent a car from Avignon for the day — the villages are 30–60 min apart and not served by trains.

🗺️ Villages

▼
🏘️
Les Baux-de-Provence

A ruined medieval citadel perched on a white limestone ridge above the plain. The ruins are dramatic and free to explore. The village below has good food and olive oil shops. January: almost no tourists, dramatic winter light.

🏘️
Gordes

The most photographed village in Provence — pale stone houses cascading down a hillside. Stunning from the valley below. Has a castle, good bakeries, and a calm off-season atmosphere in January.

🏘️
Roussillon

The ochre village — built from and surrounded by brilliant orange and red cliff formations. The colour contrast against a clear January sky is extraordinary. The ochre cliffs trail (Sentier des Ocres) is a short walk through the formations.

🏛️
Pont du Gard (Roman Aqueduct)

30 minutes from Avignon — a three-tiered Roman aqueduct bridge from 50 AD, still standing perfectly. One of the best-preserved Roman structures in the world. Free in winter, uncrowded, remarkable.

🛁 Baths

▼
♨️
Thermes de Gréoux-les-Bains

A genuine thermal spa town in the Luberon. Gréoux-les-Bains has Roman-origin sulphur springs and a working thermal centre. The town itself is a small, quiet Provençal village. About 1 hour from Avignon by car. Tattoo policy: relaxed at most public facilities.

✈️ Travel Home via Marseille
Saturday, Day 10 — Avignon → Marseille → Portland

Train from Avignon to Marseille (~35 min), then fly home. Marseille airport (MRS) connects to Paris CDG, Amsterdam, or London for transatlantic legs. If time allows, walk the Vieux-Port (old harbour) before heading to the airport.

🚂 Train: Avignon → Marseille (35 min). ✈️ MRS → CDG/AMS/LHR → PDX.

Essential Notes — Trip 3

🏰 Alhambra Tickets

Book before leaving Portland. Strict timed entry, sells out weeks ahead. alhambra-patronato.es

🚗 Car for Luberon

The Luberon villages are not accessible by public transit in January. Rent a car from Avignon for Day 9 and return it at Marseille airport on Day 10.

🌡️ Weather

Málaga/Granada: mild (10–15°C). Provence in January: cold (3–8°C), possibly frosty, but usually sunny. Bring a real coat for Avignon — the Mistral wind makes it feel colder than it is.

🚂 Train Booking

Spain: renfe.com. France: SNCF Connect app. Book the Barcelona→Avignon TGV early — it's a busy route even in winter.

🛁 Provence Thermal Towns

Beyond Gréoux, look into Digne-les-Bains (farther east, a full spa town with Alpine backdrop) if you want to extend the bath culture into a second stop.
